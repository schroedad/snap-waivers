SNAP FOOD RESTRICTION STATE DEMONSTRATION REQUEST 
Section 17(b) of the Food and Nutrition Act of 2008, as amended (the Act), allows FNS to waive 
statutory requirements of the Act to conduct pilot projects designed to test program changes to 
increase the efficiency of the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) and improve 
the delivery of SNAP benefits to eligible households. The Act limits the provisions that may be 
waived. Projects may be approved for a period of 5 years and the Act requires that each project 
must include an evaluation component that demonstrates the effects of the project. 
This template intends to guide and assist States in submitting a SNAP Food Restriction 
Demonstration Project Request. States should follow the guided prompts and questions and offer 
any additional detail, if applicable, under each section to ensure a complete understanding of the 
State’s proposed project. 
States should review the impermissible projects prohibited by Section 17 (b)(1)(B)(iv) of the Act 
and additional restrictions on demonstration projects in the appendix below before requesting a 
SNAP Food Restriction demonstration project. Additionally, to facilitate the demonstration 
review process, FNS strongly suggests States inform their Regional Office to discuss the project 
and provide any necessary technical assistance prior to submission of a request. The FNS 
National Office SNAP Food Restriction demonstration pilot team is also open to join Regional or 
State Agency calls to provide technical assistance. 
SNAP Food Restriction demonstration projects are intended to test innovative ideas that develop 
and implement modernized programmatic systems, infuse SNAP with new programmatic energy 
and vision, and strengthen State strategies to encourage healthy choices, healthy outcomes, and 
healthy families. FNS looks forward to working with States on potential changes to program 
operations that align with the Secretary’s vision for nutrition assistance programs. 
FNS reserves the right to withdraw its waiver approval and terminate demonstration projects at 
any time if FNS determines that the project is inconsistent with SNAP goals to increase the 
efficiency of the program and to improve the delivery of SNAP benefits to raise levels of 
nutrition among low-income individuals. If the State is unable to provide the data required as 
part of the terms and conditions of waiver approval or if FNS determines that the project is 
associated with significant increases in payment errors or access concerns, FNS may suspend or 
terminate the project at any time.

Statutory Citations: 
7 USC Ch. 51 §2012 (k) provides that SNAP recipients may purchase: (1) seeds and plants to 
produce food at home or (2) any food or food product for home consumption. The provision also 
bars SNAP recipients from purchasing alcoholic beverages, tobacco, and hot food products ready 
for immediate consumption. 
Regulatory Citations: 
7 CFR 271.2 “Eligible foods” defines purchases recipients may make with SNAP benefits 
aligned with the statutory citation. 
Summary of Food Restriction Demonstration Project Request: 
SNAP was created to increase access to nutritious food; however, many purchases are for foods 
that are high in added sugars and artificial sweeteners with little to no nutritional value. To 
improve the health outcomes, well-being, and quality of life for many low-income Texans, this 
waiver will prohibit the purchase of sweetened drinks and candy with SNAP benefits. 
Proposed Alternative Procedures to Operate Project: 
HHSC will help many Texas SNAP participants make good food choices by prohibiting the 
purchase of sweetened drinks and candy with SNAP benefits. Therefore, the state is requesting to 
modify the statutory and regulatory definitions of “eligible foods” to prohibit the items below. 
SNAP-Eligible Food and Food Products Amended by the Food Restriction Demonstration 
Waiver 
• Sweetened drinks: Defined as nonalcoholic beverages made with water that contain five 
grams or more of added sugar or any amount of artificial sweetener. Excluded from this 
definition are beverages that contain: (1) milk or milk products; (2) soy, rice, or similar 
milk substitutes; or (3) more than 50 percent of vegetable or fruit juice by volume. 
• Candy: Defined by Texas Administrative Code, Chapter 34, Section 3.293 (a)(4), as a 
confection made with natural or artificial sweeteners. Examples include candy bars; gum; 
drops; taffy; nuts, raisins, and fruits that have been candied, crystalized, glazed, or coated 
with chocolate, yogurt, or caramel; and nuts roasted with a sweetener. The term does not 
include products used exclusively for cooking, such as chocolate bits and cake sprinkles 
and snack items as defined by the Texas Administrative Code, Chapter 34, Section 3.293 
(a)(14).

balanced nutrition, encourages consumption of fresh fruits and vegetables, provides nutrition 
education and cooking demonstrations, supports families in making healthier choices at grocery

SNAP-Eligible Household Considerations: 
This demonstration waiver will apply to 100 percent of the Texas SNAP caseload, representing 
approximately 3.5 million people who comprise 1.6 million households. 
SNAP-Authorized Retailers Considerations: 
This demonstration project does not require retailer-level system changes. Retailers will only be 
required to restrict the purchase of prohibited items as is currently done for other ineligible items, 
like alcohol or tobacco. As there are no requirements for retailers to update or enhance their 
systems technology, all SNAP-authorized retailers in Texas will participate in the Food 
Restrictions Waiver by April of 2026.   
SNAP-Authorized Retailers Participating in the Food Restriction Demonstration Waiver 
• All retailers authorized to accept SNAP benefits STATEWIDE will participate in this 
demonstration waiver. 
Consideration of retailer size 
• Regardless of size there will be a 9-month implementation period in which all Texas 
retailers will be required to come into compliance. 
Retailer-Level System Changes 
• Texas retailers will update the ineligible items in their system to include the prohibited 
items. 
Impacts to the State Agency: 
Due to the state’s limited ability to enforce the requirements of this demonstration project 
without FNS assistance: (1) existing staff resources will be used to implement the demonstration; 
and (2) changes to systems are not needed for HHSC to operate the demonstration project. 
Therefore, this demonstration project will impact resources needed to evaluate the waiver’s 
effectiveness. This is of minimal impact to the state agency which can be absorbed with existing 
resources.

Food Restriction Pilot Compliance Plans 
Currently, HHSC in conjunction with the Texas Office of Inspector General (OIG), refers 
complaints and reports of prohibited purchases to the Food and Nutrition Service (FNS) who is 
responsible for providing penalties and disqualifications to retailers. Furthermore, there is no 
current federal mandate that FNS implement enforcing action or penalties on retailers who do not 
comply with prohibited purchase requirements mandated at the state-level. 
Staff Capacity and Training 
The project will be implemented by the department at HHSC which is responsible for monitoring 
and ensuring vendor and EBT system compliance. As such, this team already has existing 
relationships with SNAP authorized retailers and processes in place to refer retailer complaints to 
the FNS through the Texas OIG. They also have the infrastructure in place to communicate 
immediately and directly with retailers through a centralized mailbox. Furthermore, the team will 
lead implementation of this effort; communicating across departments to ensure necessary 
communication and training occur. They will also use implementation feedback to make 
continual improvements, as well as ensuring restrictions are clearly communicated and well-
known by retailers, clients, and agency staff. 
Communication Plan: 
HHSC plans to raise awareness and educate clients, the public, retailers, and staff about changes 
to allowable SNAP purchases centered around the below key messaging elements. 
• The purpose, goal, and effective date of the demonstration project. 
• Allowable and unallowable SNAP purchases. 
• SNAP basics, including who the program is for and how it works.   
• Details necessary for retailer implementation. 
Communication efforts are already underway as HHSC has started to engage retailers and 
ambassadors to help identify the requisite outreach, information needs, and training essential to 
this demonstration project. Additionally, all retailers will be able to outreach the agency with 
questions by contacting an actively monitored centralized mailbox. Furthermore, all stakeholders 
will be notified of any changes to policy through our current Gov Delivery System, which allows 
HHSC to deliver messages to subscribers.

Justification for Request: 
To better align with SNAP’s goal to help “low-income households to obtain a more nutritious 
diet,” HHSC has a responsibility to assist households purchase food with nutritional value. 
Currently, the rise in the number of residents, both children and adults, who are overweight, and 
struggle with obesity, is a major concern for Texas. According to the American Heart Association, 
obesity in Texas is an epidemic that without intervention is projected to increase from 34 percent 
of Texas adults to 53 percent by 2030.1 More importantly, obesity challenges can make a person 
more susceptible to heart disease, stroke, high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, and certain cancers; 
causing long-term economic consequences to Texas’ health care system.2

The United States Department of Agriculture’s (USDA’s) dietary guide states, “When added 
sugars in foods and beverages exceed 10 percent of calories, a healthy dietary pattern within calorie 
limits is very difficult to achieve.”3 Furthermore, the USDA states, “sugar contains calories and 
only calories; it provides no other nutrients - no protein, no vitamins and no minerals.”4 As there 
is a known link between sugar consumption and obesity, HHSC is ensuring the taxpayer funded 
benefit subsidizes food items which provide real nutritional value, rather than empty calories 
which primarily lead to chronic health issues.   
Proposed Evaluation Procedures: 
The evaluation will consist of a holistic analysis focused on gathering feedback from retailers, 
participants, and key stakeholders. HHSC will also analyze complaint data from both participants 
and retailers. HHSC will leverage existing resources through our SNAP-Ed program and 
partnerships, as available, and may modify the current evaluator agreement to supplement 
procedures to implement this waiver. 
Participant Surveys 
To analyze changes to SNAP participant behavior, HHSC will conduct three surveys: (1) prior to 
the project’s implementation, (2) mid-way through the project’s implementation, and (3) 
following the project’s initial expiration date. These surveys will gather feedback to determine 
whether there are changes in the amount of unhealthy foods family’s purchase, changes in the 
number of meals eaten outside the home or not purchased with SNAP benefits, increases in the 
amount of non-SNAP dollars spent on the purchase of restricted food items, project awareness, 
and whether households can identify which food items are prohibited. Furthermore, the project 
will collect statements from households on the overall impact of the program to gather any 
unexpected impacts. 
Participant Data 
HHSC will collect and analyze client complaints about this demonstration, as well as review out­
of-state transaction data to determine whether there are any changes in cross-border shopping 
transactions. 
Retailer Surveys 
Three retailer surveys will be conducted at the same time as the participant surveys. These 
surveys will assess whether retailers feel aware of and knowledgeable about allowable 
purchases, collect practices used to inform clients of unallowable purchases, measure retailer 
satisfaction, and analyze any changes to operations retailers believe resulted from this project.

Appendix 
Impermissible Projects 
Section 17(b)(1)(B)(iv) of the Act explicitly prohibits waivers of certain provisions of the Act. 
FNS is unable to approve projects that involve a waiver of these provisions. 
Impermissible eligibility criteria waivers 
Food Restriction demonstration projects may not: 
• Change the definition of household for those living in Federally subsidized housing for 
older adults, group living arrangements, domestic violence shelters, homeless shelters, 
and drug and alcohol treatment centers), institutions, or boarding houses (Sec. 3(m)(4) 
and (5)); 
• Change the gross income standards of eligibility for households that do not have an 
elderly or disabled member to a level other than 130 percent of the Federal Poverty Level 
(Sec. 5(c)(2)); 
• Change the work requirements exemption for parents or household members caring for 
with dependent children under the age of 6 or caring for an incapacitated person (Sec. 
6(d)(2)(B)); 
• Increase the shelter deduction for households with low or no out-of-pocket housing costs; 
or 
• Deny benefits to an otherwise eligible individual or household (last sentence of Sec. 
5(a)). 
Impermissible State operations waivers 
Food Restriction demonstration projects may not: 
• Waive the requirements (Sec. 11(e)(2)(B)) for States to: 
o Provide timely, accurate and fair service to SNAP applicants and participants; 
o Develop a SNAP application; and if the State has a website, make the application 
available on their website in every language a printed application is available; 
o Allow a household to apply on the same day they first contact a SNAP office 
during office hours; 
o Consider an application with only name, address, and signature to be filed on the 
date of application; 
o Require an adult representative to certify that the information on the application is 
true and that all members are citizens or eligible aliens; 
o Provide a method of certifying and issuing benefits to homeless households; or 
o Determine applicant eligibility within 30 days of the date of the filing of an 
application (time standard in Sec. 11(e)(3)). 
• Change the provisions outlining which parts of the Act are not allowed to be waived; 
• Absolve a State from acting with reasonable promptness on substantial reported changes 
in income or household size;

FNS is available to answer questions and provide technical assistance to States requesting 
demonstration projects. If you are unsure whether your State’s request would require an 
impermissible waiver, please contact FNS. 
Additional Restrictions on Demonstration Projects 
• Demonstration projects may be approved for up to five years with extensions possible 
thereafter. 
• If a demonstration project reduces benefits by more than 20 percent for more than 5 
percent of households in the project area (excluding households whose benefits are 
reduced for failure to comply with work requirements), the demonstration project: 
o cannot affect more than 15% of households in the State AND 
o may not continue for more than 5 years unless the Secretary approves an 
extension request. 
• Demonstration projects may not: 
o Provide benefits in the form of cash or a manner otherwise non-restricted to food 
(except for a project approved before August 22, 1996); 
o Allow SNAP funds to be used to fund other public assistance programs, or use the 
funds for any purpose other than the purchase of food, program administration, or 
employment and training activities; or 
o Count SNAP benefits as income or resources for tax purposes, welfare, public 
assistance programs or any other Federal, State, or local assistance program (Sec. 
8(b)).
